Designing an AI agent that helps marketers write content that ranks

This project focused on designing an enriched SEO Agent that helps content marketers produce articles that are substantive enough to rank, transparent enough to trust, and structured enough to publish without major edits.

  • 3 rounds of usability testing
  • Working AI agent built for testing
  • Prototype shipped in production codebase

As a product designer on the team, I led the UX and UI design of the SEO agent while collaborating with a lead product designer, a product manager, and a lead user researcher.

Brief

SEO-optimized pages were the top requested AI use case

The UX research team spoke with 15 content marketers and found that SEO is not just about creating more content. Companies invest in a CMS, pay freelancers, build content calendars, but none of it matters if no one clicks on the pages.

Existing AI writing tools make this worse. You enter a keyword and instantly get an article, but with no visibility into what sources were used or why this specific structure was chosen. Marketers wanted AI that helps them rank, not just write.

Understanding workflow

One marketer used 18 agents just to get a decent article

When I interviewed one of our internal content marketers, I expected a simple workflow. Instead, he showed me 18 agents in his Clay setup. Each one handled a different piece of the SEO process, but together they crafted an article that had the right structure, the right depth, and actually matched what he had in mind.

The other marketer relied on a freelancer and manual review in Notion. These two workflows were different but underlined the same need: to trust the output before it was written, not fix it after.

A marketer's Clay workspace: 18 chained power agents to produce one SEO article

Opportunity

Give marketers control before the AI writes, not after

Hypothesis

Marketers would rather spend time making sure the AI has all the information it needs upfront than receive a full article and spend hours reading and correcting it.

The SEO agent could break content creation into three transparent steps: research, outline, then article.

Each step gives the marketer a checkpoint to review and approve before the AI moves forward.

How I worked

Prototyping with AI, not just for AI

I wanted every test to feel real. Not a clickable Figma mockup, but something users could actually interact with, type into, and react to.

So I built a working SEO agent in Langchain's Agent Builder. It analyzed competitors, identified content gaps, and generated outlines from real data. This meant my first user test was run on actual AI output, not placeholder text.

For the interface, I replicated Prismic's UI in Lovable. Users were testing inside something that looked and felt like their real product, not a prototype. That is what gave the feedback its depth. They were reacting to interactions, not just layouts.

When the design was validated, I used Claude Code to rebuild the prototype directly in Prismic's codebase. Engineering received a working version, not a spec to interpret.

Design Exploration 1

I assumed research would be a background step. Users proved me wrong.

I designed an agent that analyses top-ranking pages for a given keyword: why they rank well, what topics they still miss, and what questions people ask. It then turns this into a structured outline and then into an article.

The first test was informal. I sat with a content marketer at Prismic and showed him the raw agent output.

I assumed he would quickly skim the research and focus on the outline, but he actually deep-dived into reading the research step.

I had designed the competitive research as a background process, but in reality users wanted it visible. I rebuilt the UX flow to put it front and centre, where marketers can review, and even edit and add to the research.

"I can see all the steps it is taking in a granular level. It gives me the confidence that AI did its job. I would say what I would want is the chance to kind of look at those things, being able to edit and add stuff."

Design Exploration 2

Making SEO hygiene go on auto-pilot

Adding internal and external link suggestions to the outline

Both users flagged link-building as a high-value, high-effort task they often skip.

At first I put linking suggestions at the end of the flow, mirroring what I had seen on Clay. But user tests revealed this was too late. By then the structure was set and links felt like an afterthought.

I moved link suggestions into the outline step. Users can accept or reject each suggestion before the article is written.

Current Status

It's in development!

The SEO agent's future

The feature is currently being built by the engineering team. For smoother development handover, I replicated the fully interactive prototype directly in the Prismic codebase using Claude Code.

One need that surfaced during testing and remains to be addressed: how to update already existing content.

KPIs to look for

The metric to watch post-launch: how much time marketers spend between starting an article and hitting publish. If the agent is doing its job, that number should drop significantly without sacrificing the quality of what ranks.
